## PART 1: CREATIVE BRIEF OVERVIEW

### Project Overview

Design a complete brand identity and marketing system for **Bookmark**, a book recommendation platform that helps readers remember why they wanted to read something and how it made them feel. Unlike algorithmic recommendation services that trap readers in filter bubbles, Bookmark captures the context around reading—who recommended it, what mood you were seeking, the feeling it gave you—so readers build a meaningful relationship with their reading life instead of just consuming and forgetting.

**The Physical Component**: Bookmark includes a **smart reading light with book-recognition technology**—a rechargeable LED reading light (approximately 6"x9" illuminated panel) with page-holding clips and embedded camera/OCR technology. This device automatically recognizes which book you're reading, tracks pages read and reading speed, and syncs seamlessly with the recommendation app via Bluetooth.

Students will create white-label branding for a recommendation engine platform that combines:
- **Smart Reading Light** (physical IoT device with book recognition)
- **Preference-Learning App** (digital recommendation engine)
- **Unified Reading Profile** (physical + digital books tracked together)

The challenge is designing for readers who love books but struggle to remember what they've read, why they added books to their TBR list, and whether recommendations from various sources actually match their taste.

> **DESIGN CHALLENGE #1**: How do you make a camera-equipped reading light feel **helpful**, not **surveillance-y**? (Hint: Amazon's Ring cameras failed this. Apple's Face ID succeeded. What's the difference?)

### Company Background: Founder Story

Jessica had read 200+ books in four years but couldn't remember half of them. She'd pick up an author's newest release and realize halfway through she'd already read their previous book—couldn't remember if she'd liked it. Her Goodreads was full of 4-star ratings with no context. Amazon kept recommending the same thrillers. She'd screenshot book recommendations from podcasts, Instagram, friends' texts, then forget where she'd seen them or why they sounded compelling.

At book club, someone asked "What's the best book you've read this year?" She froze. Forty-seven books that year. Couldn't pick one—not because they were all equal, but because she couldn't remember the **feeling** they gave her.

Goodreads reviews felt like homework. Notes app was chaos. Manual reading journal attempts failed because she didn't want to write essays.

She started using physical bookmarks and writing on them: book title, date finished, one sentence about the feeling, who recommended it. Simple. It worked. Friends saw her bookmark collection and wanted the system.

*"You don't need to remember everything about a book. You need to remember how it made you feel and whether to trust the person who recommended it."*

Bookmark became that system—**smart reading light** for automatic physical book tracking, **digital app** for recommendations and discovery, both working together so readers build a reading life they actually remember.

## PART 2: PRODUCT DEEP DIVE

### What Bookmark Is

A recommendation engine that learns your reading preferences and suggests books based on patterns in what you've loved—but unlike algorithmic platforms, Bookmark captures **context** (who recommended it, why you added it, what mood you were seeking) and **feeling** (emotional response, memorable moments, who else would love it). The system includes a **smart reading light** (physical IoT device) and **preference-learning app** (digital interface) that work together to build reading memory **automatically**.

### TECHNICAL CONTEXT FOR DESIGNERS (OOC)

**The "Magic" Layer**: The smart reading light's book recognition technology represents aspirational 2026-2028 capabilities. Current OCR and computer vision technology could theoretically achieve this, but real-world implementation would face challenges (lighting conditions, camera angle, book condition, font recognition).

**For this project**: Design as if the technology works seamlessly. Your programming teammates may implement a simplified version (manual book entry, barcode scanning, or Kindle-only tracking). Your job is to design the **premium vision** of what Bookmark could be—not to engineer around current limitations.

**Design accordingly**: Your packaging, UI, and marketing should communicate "smart book-recognition technology" without making promises about 100% accuracy. Think: how does Alexa marketing handle imperfect voice recognition? How does Roomba marketing acknowledge it won't catch every crumb?

### Physical Component: Smart Reading Light & Page Holder

**Format:** LED reading light with page-holding clips (approximately 6"x9" illuminated panel) | Rechargeable via USB-C | Bluetooth connectivity | Embedded book-recognition technology

**Base Product Reference:** Similar to flat-panel LED reading lights (students can reference existing products like rechargeable book lights on Walmart/Amazon for design mockups)

**Fictitious Smart Features (The "Magic"):**

- **Embedded camera/sensor** "reads" physical book pages via OCR and book recognition technology
- **Automatic tracking:** Recognizes which book you're reading, tracks pages read, reading speed, session duration
- **Bluetooth sync to app:** Sends reading data to Bookmark app in real-time
- **Unified reading profile:** Also connects to Kindle, Apple Books, Kobo, Google Play Books accounts—tracks ALL reading (physical + digital) in one place
- **Tactile reading controls:** Touch-sensitive panel to mark quotes, flag pages, add quick notes without phone

**Physical Features:**

- **Page-holding clips** keep book open hands-free
- **Adjustable LED brightness** (warm/cool light settings for day/night reading)
- **Slim profile** fits in book bag, doesn't add bulk
- **Long battery life** (20+ hours reading time per charge)
- **Durable materials** for daily use (bedside, commute, travel)

**Why Smart Reading Light Works:**

- Solves biggest gap in reading apps: tracking physical book reading automatically
- Practical function (lights pages, holds book open) + smart technology layer
- No manual entry required (camera recognizes book, tracks progress passively)
- Unified profile means recommendations based on ALL reading, not just digital
- Premium object that enhances reading experience while gathering data
- Students can easily mockup using existing LED reading light products as reference

### Digital Component: Recommendation App

**Core Features:**

- **Smart TBR Management:** Import from screenshots, podcasts, social media | Tag by mood/genre/source | Sort by priority/added date
- **Reading Tracker:** Currently reading progress | Time-to-finish estimates | Reading streak tracking (syncs automatically from device)
- **Recommendation Engine:** Learns from completed books, mood patterns, trusted sources | Suggests books outside filter bubble | "If you loved X, try Y because [specific reason]"
- **Source Analysis:** Tracks which recommenders have highest hit rate for you | Shows alignment percentage with your taste
- **Memory Prompts:** "You finished this 6 months ago—remember how you felt?" | "You added this because [saved reason]"
- **Discovery Mode:** Intentionally shows books outside your usual genres based on feeling/mood match (see "How Discovery Mode Works" below)

**Advanced Features:**

- **Reading Goals:** Monthly/yearly targets | Genre diversity tracking | Reading time analytics
- **Quick Reflection Capture:** One-sentence feeling prompts (not full reviews) | Voice note integration for hands-free capture
- **Social (Optional):** Share recommendations with trusted circle | Private book club groups | No public reviews/ratings
- **Library Integration:** Check availability at local libraries | Indie bookstore partnership links

### How Discovery Mode Works (Technical Logic)

**The Challenge**: Recommend books outside filter bubbles without random suggestions.

**Bookmark's Approach**: Cross-genre pattern matching based on reader-described feelings.

**Data Sources**:
- **Your patterns**: You've described 6 books as "beautiful sentences"—the app notes authors, genres, writing styles, pacing (from reading light data: did you savor slowly or rush?).
- **Aggregate patterns**: Other readers who loved those same 6 books ALSO loved [Book X] and described it similarly—even though it's sci-fi, not literary fiction.
- **Mood/feeling tags**: Readers tag books with feelings ("made me cry," "couldn't put down," "changed how I think"), not just genres. App matches feeling patterns across genres.

**Example**:
- Aisha loves *The Overstory* (literary fiction, "beautiful sentences")
- App sees: Other readers who loved *The Overstory* also loved *The Left Hand of Darkness* (sci-fi) and used the same "beautiful sentences" descriptor
- Discovery Mode suggests: "Try *Left Hand of Darkness*—it's sci-fi, but readers who loved *The Overstory* describe it the same way you describe your favorites."

**Why This Works**: Breaks genre silos by matching emotional response patterns, not surface categories.

### How Physical + Digital Work Together

**Before Reading:**

- **App:** Add book to TBR with context (who recommended, why, mood tags)
- **Reading Light:** Already charged and ready at bedside/reading spot

**During Reading (Physical Books):**

- **Reading Light:** Clip onto book, turn on light, camera automatically recognizes book title and author
- **Passive Tracking:** Device tracks pages read, reading speed, session time—sends to app via Bluetooth
- **Quick Notes:** Touch panel to flag memorable passages (syncs page numbers to app for later reference)

**During Reading (Digital Books):**

- **App:** Connects to Kindle/Apple Books/Kobo accounts, pulls reading data automatically
- **Unified Profile:** Physical book reading + digital reading combined in one reading history

**After Finishing a Book:**

- **App:** Prompts: "You finished [Book]! One-sentence feeling?" | Rate it | Who'd love this? | What to read next?
- **Reading Light:** Data already synced—app knows you finished based on pages tracked
- **Pattern Learning:** App analyzes: You loved books you described as "beautiful sentences"—here are similar books

**Recommendation Flow:**

- App learns patterns from ALL reading (physical + digital): pacing preferences, genre variety, author styles, "feeling" patterns from your one-sentence reflections
- Suggests books based on: "You loved [Book A] because 'couldn't put down'—try [Book B], readers describe it the same way"
- Reading light provides data richness (did you race through it or savor slowly?), app provides intelligence

**The Integration Advantage:**

- No manual logging for physical books (camera does the work)
- Digital books sync automatically (connected accounts)
- One complete reading profile powers better recommendations
- Reading light enhances experience (better lighting, hands-free) while gathering preference data

### Pricing & Business Model (For Context)

**Smart Reading Light**: $89.99 retail (premium but accessible—compare to: $129 Kindle Paperwhite, $99 premium book lights don't exist, $15 basic clip lights)

**App Pricing**:
- **Free tier**: Basic TBR management, manual book logging, simple recommendations
- **Premium tier** ($4.99/month or $39.99/year): Device sync, Discovery Mode, source analysis, unlimited reading history, library integration

**Revenue Model**:
- Device sales (one-time)
- Premium subscriptions (recurring)
- Affiliate partnerships (indie bookstores, Libro.fm, Bookshop.org—commission on book sales)
- Potential B2B (book clubs, libraries, publishers)

**Positioning**: Premium tool for serious readers—priced below Kindle but above novelty gadgets. Comparable to: quality headphones, nice journal, cooking gadget you use daily.

**Design Implication**: Packaging and materials should signal "worth $90," not "$30 with tech gimmick markup." Think: Moleskine meets Apple AirPods.

### Why This Works When Others Don't

**Goodreads:** Public performance, star ratings without context, algorithm serves Amazon not readers, reviews from strangers with unknown taste alignment.

**Amazon:** Recommendation engine optimized for sales not reading satisfaction, filter bubble traps you in similar books, no source tracking, no feeling/mood capture.

**StoryGraph:** Better mood tracking but purely digital (no physical book auto-tracking), limited source attribution, recommendation algorithm still opaque.

**BookTok/Bookstagram:** Hype-driven, no personalization, recommendations disappear in feed, can't track which influencers match your taste.

**Personal Systems (Notes App/Spreadsheets):** No recommendation engine, purely manual, screenshots get lost, no pattern recognition across books.

**Bookmark Difference:** Combines automatic physical book tracking (smart device) with digital intelligence (pattern recognition, source analysis, discovery algorithms that serve readers not platforms). Captures **why** you wanted to read something so you make better decisions about **what** to read next. **No manual logging required.**

## PART 3: USER RESEARCH

### User Story 1: Maya, 32, Marketing Manager

Maya reads 3-4 books monthly, mostly contemporary fiction and memoirs. Income ~$58K. Commutes via train, reads before bed. Her problem: she screenshots book recommendations from podcasts and Instagram, then forgets which episode or account suggested them. Her camera roll has 200+ book screenshots with zero context. She'll buy a book months later because the cover looks familiar, not remembering if the source was trustworthy. Her "system" is chaos—Goodreads TBR has 400 books, no priority order, no memory of why she added them. She wishes she could remember who recommended books that became favorites so she could trust that source again.

**How Bookmark helps:** App lets her tag screenshots with source while saving them. After finishing books, she uses one-sentence feeling prompts. After 3 months, app's source analysis shows that "Forever35" podcast has 85% hit rate while BookTok account has 20%. She trusts the data, focuses on high-value sources, stops wasting time on hyped books that aren't for her.

**Device Interaction Moment:** Maya clips the Bookmark light onto her book for the first time—it recognizes the title instantly, no typing, no scanning a barcode. She taps the touch panel to flag a beautiful passage. The app saves the page number automatically. This is the "just works" experience she expects from her AirPods, now for her reading life.

**Design takeaway:** Visual system needs to make source tracking feel essential, not extra work—show success rate of recommenders prominently.

### User Story 2: David, 41, Software Engineer

David reads 2-3 books monthly across genres—sci-fi, business, history, literary fiction. Income ~$72K. Married, two kids. His problem: he'll finish a book and someone asks "Was it good?" and he can't articulate why he liked it beyond "Yeah, it was good." He's read 150+ books in past three years but remembers maybe 20 with any detail. He tried Goodreads reviews but writing paragraphs felt like homework he'd avoid. App uses these patterns to suggest books: "You've loved 6 books you described as 'couldn't put down'—try this." He builds useful reading memory without essay-writing.

Tomorrow morning, the app prompts: "One sentence about how it felt?" He types it while drinking coffee. The device made tracking effortless—no interrupting the reading flow.

**Design takeaway:** Emphasize "quick capture" language—this isn't a book report, it's memory insurance. Device should feel invisible during reading, helpful after.

Aisha reads 4-5 books monthly, wants to read more diversely but algorithms trap her. Income ~$51K. Active in book club, follows book accounts. His problem: students ask for recommendations and he blanks on details—"There was this great book about… I can't remember the title." He's read incredible books but can't recall them when it matters. His Goodreads has dates and star ratings, nothing about why a book would work for specific students. He wants to be the teacher who gives perfect recommendations but his memory fails him.

## PART 4: STRATEGIC GUIDANCE

### Visual Differentiation Strategy

**Current Market Territory**:
- **Goodreads/Amazon**: Cluttered, dated web design, overwhelming star-rating visuals
- **StoryGraph**: Playful, illustrated, skews young (great for YA, less for literary readers)
- **Literal**: Clean minimalist, cold tech aesthetic, lacks personality
- **Book Instagram**: Feminine-coded aesthetics (florals, pastels, hand-lettering, cozy vibes)

**Bookmark's Visual Opportunity**:
**Sophisticated literary design meets premium tech product**

Think: *New Yorker* meets Apple product photography. *Paris Review* meets Sonos packaging. Strand Bookstore tote meets AirPods Pro case.

**Characteristics**:
- **Typography**: Serif headlines (literary credibility) + clean sans body (digital readability)
- **Color palette**: Rich, saturated but not bright—think book covers from A24 films, Criterion Collection, independent publishers (New Directions, Graywolf, Coffee House Press)
- **Photography**: Hands holding books + devices, natural light, lived-in reading spaces, diverse representation (race, age, reading contexts—not just white women in bathtubs)
- **Tone**: Intelligent without being pretentious, warm without being cutesy, tech-forward without being cold

**What to Avoid**:
- Illustrated books with faces (too StoryGraph)
- Pastels and florals (too BookTok aesthetic)
- Corporate blue gradients (too Amazon/Goodreads)
- Sans-serif everything (too startup generic)
- Overly minimalist (too cold for book lovers)

**Key Question**: Would this design feel at home in an independent bookstore? Would a reader trust it to understand *their* taste in books?

### Collaboration with Programming Students

**What you need from them:**

- **Book recognition technology specs**: How does camera/OCR identify books? Accuracy rates? Lighting requirements? Optimal camera angle and distance from page?
- **Bluetooth connectivity implementation**: Pairing process, data sync frequency, battery impact? How does continuous connection affect device battery life?
- **Digital reading account API integration**: Which services allow data access? Kindle, Apple Books, Kobo capabilities? API limitations?
- **Reading tracking algorithm**: How distinguish active reading from device being left on? Page turn detection methods? How calculate reading speed?
- **Device battery optimization**: How long between charges with typical reading usage? Impact of continuous Bluetooth, camera OCR processing, LED brightness on battery?
- **Privacy & data storage**: Where is reading data stored? Local device, cloud sync? How ensure camera only captures OCR data, not images?

**What they need from you:**

- **Complete industrial design specifications**:
  - Device dimensions and weight budget (affects battery capacity, reader comfort)
  - Camera placement and field of view (affects book recognition accuracy, reading ergonomics)
  - LED panel design and light distribution pattern (affects reading comfort, eye strain)
  - Touch control placement and gesture library (affects interrupt-free reading flow)
  - Page clip mechanism design (affects compatibility with book sizes, concern about book damage)
  - Material specifications (affects manufacturing cost, durability, premium feel)
  - Button/control tactility (physical buttons vs. touch-sensitive, feedback mechanisms)

- **Brand identity assets**:
  - Logo files (vector, for device branding and app UI)
  - Color codes (for LED indicator lights, app interface, packaging)
  - Typography system (for app UI, device display if applicable)
  - Iconography library (for touch controls, app interface, status indicators)

- **User experience flows**:
  - Device pairing process (unboxing → charging → Bluetooth pairing → first book recognition)
  - Book recognition feedback (visual/haptic confirmation when book is recognized, error states)
  - Error state handling (book not recognized, low battery, connection lost, poor lighting)
  - Privacy indicators (camera active light, data sync confirmation, user control over data)
  - First-time user onboarding (how introduce device capabilities without overwhelming?)

**How to work together:**

Regular check-ins on technical constraints that affect design. Ask questions early about feasibility: Can camera really recognize any book? How accurate is OCR in various lighting? Which digital reading platforms allow API access? Will Bluetooth stay connected continuously or sync periodically?

Share work-in-progress for feedback. Be open to technical limitations requiring design adaptation (maybe camera needs optimal lighting conditions, maybe some e-reader accounts can't sync, maybe battery life requires periodic charging not continuous use).

Explain design rationale: Why device needs to feel premium not novelty, why camera placement matters for natural reading position, why touch controls should be intuitive for readers holding book, why privacy indicators build trust.

**Critical design constraint**: The device must feel like it **enhances** reading (better light, hands-free pages), not **interrupts** reading (distracting tech, manual input). Every industrial design decision should ask: "Would this make me want to use it tonight?"

They're building the recognition engine and connectivity, you're designing the physical reading experience enhancement—both essential.
