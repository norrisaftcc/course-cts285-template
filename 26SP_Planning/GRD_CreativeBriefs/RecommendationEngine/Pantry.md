## PART 1: CREATIVE BRIEF OVERVIEW

### Project Overview

Design a complete brand identity and marketing system for **Pantry**, a recipe recommendation platform that suggests meals based on ingredients you actually have—eliminating “what’s for dinner?” paralysis, reducing food waste, and ending recipe searches for dishes you can’t make because you’re missing half the ingredients. Unlike recipe apps showing aspirational meals or grocery delivery apps pushing purchases, Pantry uses a smart kitchen display with barcode scanner to track your real inventory and recommend recipes you can cook right now with what’s already in your kitchen.

Students will create white-label branding for a recommendation engine platform that combines a **preference-learning app** with a **portable smart kitchen display**. The challenge is designing for home cooks (beginners to intermediate, individuals to families) who struggle with meal planning, waste food that expires before use, can’t remember what’s in their pantry, and face daily “what’s for dinner?” decision fatigue.

### Target Audience

**Demographics:** Ages 25-45 | Income $35K-$75K | Urban/suburban | Mix of singles, couples, young families | Rent or own | Cook at home 4-6 nights per week

**Cooking Behavior:** Intermediate home cooks (can follow recipes, not culinary experts) | Grocery shop weekly or bi-weekly | Use mix of fresh ingredients + pantry staples | Cook weeknight dinners (30-45 min) + occasional weekend projects | Follow recipes from apps, websites, cookbooks | Want to cook more but face decision paralysis

**Pain Points:**
Can’t remember what ingredients are in pantry/fridge (buy duplicates or assume they have something they don’t) | Food expires before they use it (throwing away wilted produce, expired dairy, forgotten leftovers) | “What’s for dinner?” decision takes 20+ minutes (scrolling recipes, realizing missing ingredients, giving up) | Find great recipe online, missing 3-4 ingredients, can’t make it tonight | Shopping list chaos (forget to write things down, run out of staples unexpectedly) | Recipe rut (make same 7 meals repeatedly because they know they have ingredients) | Meal planning feels like homework (too much mental energy after work) | Grocery shopping inefficient (forget what’s at home, buy what’s already stocked)

**Current Behavior:**
Check pantry/fridge multiple times while meal planning (still forget items) | Screenshot recipes, lose track of them, can’t find when ready to cook | Google “recipes with chicken” and scroll endlessly | Start recipe, realize missing ingredient, improvise or give up | Throw away expired food weekly (guilt about waste but can’t seem to prevent it) | Keep mental shopping list, forget items at store | Default to delivery/takeout when can’t decide (even when wanting to cook)

**What They Want:**
Know what ingredients they have without checking cupboards constantly | Recipe suggestions using actual inventory (not fantasy pantry) | Stop wasting food that expires before use | Decide “what’s for dinner?” in under 2 minutes | Shopping list that builds itself as items run low | Use up ingredients before they go bad | Try new recipes without buying 15 new ingredients | Reduce food waste guilt and save money

### Company Background: Founder Story

Maya stood in her kitchen at 6:30 PM on a Wednesday, tired from work, staring into her fridge. Half a bell pepper (going soft), chicken breasts, some spinach (wilting), random condiments. She knew she had pasta somewhere. Maybe rice? Beans? She couldn’t remember.

She Googled “chicken recipes.” Endless scroll. First one needed coconut milk, ginger, lime—don’t have it. Second needed white wine, mushrooms, cream—don’t have it. Third looked perfect until “marinate for 4 hours.” It was 6:30 PM.

She gave up. Ordered Thai food. Again. While waiting for delivery, she cleaned out the fridge. Threw away wilted spinach, soft bell pepper, expired yogurt, forgotten leftovers. $30 of food in the trash. This happened every week.

*I’m throwing away food while ordering delivery because I can’t figure out what to cook with what I have.*

She started photographing her pantry, keeping lists on her phone. It lasted two days. Too much manual work. She needed automation. She mocked up a kitchen display that scanned groceries automatically, tracked inventory, suggested recipes using those ingredients, alerted when food was expiring.

Friends saw it. “I need this.” “I threw away so much food last week.” “I can never remember what’s in my pantry.”

*“We’re wasting food and money because we can’t remember what we have and don’t know what to cook with it.”*

Pantry became that system—a smart display tracking real inventory automatically, answering “what’s for dinner?” based on what you actually have.

## PART 2: PRODUCT DEEP DIVE

### What Pantry Is

A recipe recommendation platform that tracks your actual pantry and fridge inventory via a smart kitchen display with built-in scanner. Scan your grocery receipt (all items added at once) or scan items individually, and Pantry automatically tracks what you have, suggests recipes using those ingredients, alerts when food is about to expire, and builds your shopping list as items run low. The display mounts on your fridge or counter as home base, then becomes portable for step-by-step cooking guidance—bringing recipes to your prep area or stove with large text, timers, and hands-free controls.

### Physical Component: Smart Kitchen Display with Barcode Scanner

**Format:** Touchscreen tablet-style display (8-10” diagonal) | Built-in barcode/receipt scanner (top-mounted camera) | Dual-mode: mountable + portable | Rechargeable battery | WiFi connectivity

**Base Product Reference:** Similar to Amazon Echo Show, Google Nest Hub, iPad Mini (students can reference smart displays and small tablets for design mockups, but kitchen-ruggedized)

**Fictitious Smart Features (The “Magic”):**

**Scanning & Inventory:**

- **Receipt scanning:** Scan entire grocery receipt, OCR reads all items, adds to inventory in one scan (way faster than scanning individual barcodes)
- **Individual barcode scanning:** Point item at camera for items bought without receipt (farmers market, corner store, already-owned items)
- **Product database:** 500,000+ grocery products (brand, name, category, typical expiration)
- **Expiration tracking:** Scanner reads “best by” dates OR estimates based on product type
- **Quantity tracking:** Counts items (“2 cans tomatoes, 1 bag rice, 3 bell peppers”)
- **Manual entry:** Touchscreen or voice (“Add 5 potatoes”) for items without barcodes
- **Saved recipe shortcuts:** Frequently cooked recipes saved as one-tap buttons | Tap “Made Spaghetti” → all spaghetti ingredients auto-deducted from inventory without scanning | Learns your go-to meals, makes updates instant

**Recipe Recommendations:**

- **Based on real inventory:** “You have chicken, rice, bell peppers—here are 3 recipes”
- **Completion percentage:** “You have 8/10 ingredients for Stir Fry (missing: soy sauce, ginger)”
- **Expiration prioritization:** “Bell peppers expire in 2 days—use them first”
- **Time filtering:** Weeknight quick (30 min) vs. weekend projects (1+ hour)
- **Dietary preferences:** Vegetarian, gluten-free, dairy-free, low-carb
- **Learning algorithm:** You never cook fish → stops suggesting seafood

**Shopping List Automation:**

- **Auto-adds low-stock items:** “Eggs: 0 remaining” → shopping list
- **Recipe-based adding:** Need soy sauce for selected recipe → one-tap adds to list
- **Staples tracking:** Learns you always keep milk, bread, coffee → alerts when low
- **Store organization:** List sorted by grocery store sections
- **Syncs to phone:** Shopping list on display + mobile app for store

**Mounted Mode (Home Base):**

- **Magnetic mount on fridge** OR **countertop charging dock** OR **wall bracket**
- **Always charging:** Device charges when docked
- **Hands-free:** Home screen shows recipe suggestions, alerts, shopping list
- **Scanning station:** Unpack groceries near display, scan receipt or items

**Portable Mode (Cooking Companion):**

- **Detachable:** Lift off mount (magnetic or slide-off)
- **Lightweight:** <1.5 lbs, one-handed carry
- **Built-in kickstand:** Props on counter hands-free
- **4-6 hour battery:** Full cooking session
- **Splash-resistant:** Kitchen-safe, wipeable screen
- **Recipe mode:** Step-by-step instructions, large text, auto-timers, voice control

**Physical Features:**

- Slim profile (~0.5” thick) | Durable kitchen-safe materials | Rubberized edges (grip + drop protection) | Touchscreen (moisture-responsive) | Scanner LED indicator | Speaker + microphone (voice control) | Pantry branding

**Why This Works:**
Lives where decisions happen | Eliminates manual tracking (scan receipt = done) | Automatic waste prevention (expiration alerts) | Portable for cooking (not just planning) | One device replaces recipe apps, shopping lists, timers | Saved recipe shortcuts make frequent meals instant | Students can reference existing smart displays for design

### Digital Component: Pantry Mobile App

**Core Features:**

- **Synced Shopping List:** Mirror of display’s list | Check off at store | “Do I have this?” search prevents duplicates
- **Recipe Library:** Full recipe database filterable by inventory | “Show recipes I can make now” vs. “Show recipes missing 1-2 ingredients”
- **Remote Inventory Check:** At grocery store, check if you have item at home
- **Meal Planning:** Weekly view, drag recipes to days, auto-generates shopping list for week
- **Dietary Filters:** Save preferences (vegetarian household, gluten-free member, etc.)

**Advanced Features:**

- **Meal Prep Mode:** Multi-recipe planning for batch cooking | Shows shared ingredients across recipes | “Cook these 3 recipes Sunday, eat all week”
- **Leftover Tracking:** “Made chicken, have 2 servings left” → suggestions using leftover chicken
- **Serving Size Adjustment:** Recipe serves 4, you need 6 → auto-adjusts ingredient quantities
- **Nutrition Info:** Calories, macros per serving (pulled from ingredient database)
- **Cost Tracking:** Approximate meal cost based on ingredient prices | “This recipe costs ~$12 for 4 servings”
- **Family Sharing:** Multiple users on one account | Partner adds items via phone, syncs to display

### How Physical + Digital Work Together

**After Grocery Shopping:**

- Unpack groceries on counter near display | Place receipt on scanner camera → beep → all items added (30 seconds total) | OR scan individual items if no receipt | Fresh produce without barcodes: tap screen “Add 3 bell peppers” or speak it | Display confirms: “42 items in inventory”

**Meal Planning (Weeknight 6pm):**

- Stand in kitchen, look at display | Home screen shows: “Tonight’s Picks (30 min or less)” - Stir-fry (uses expiring bell peppers), Pasta primavera, Chicken tacos | Alert: “⚠️ Bell peppers expire in 2 days” | Tap Stir-fry to see full recipe

**Cooking:**

- Lift display off mount | Carry to stove/prep counter | Tap “Start Cooking” | Step-by-step: dice chicken → heat pan → add ingredients → timer auto-starts | Voice: “Next step” advances | Kickstand props hands-free | Screen gets splattered, wipe clean, keep going | After cooking: tap “Made Stir-Fry” → bell peppers, chicken, rice auto-deducted from inventory

**Using Saved Recipe Shortcut:**

- Making spaghetti again (third time this month) | Don’t need step-by-step | Tap “Made Spaghetti” button on home screen | Pasta, sauce, garlic, basil auto-removed from inventory | No scanning, instant update

**Shopping List Build-Up:**

- Over the week: eggs run out → auto-adds to list | Selected recipe needs soy sauce (don’t have) → tap “Add to list” | Milk getting low → alerts, auto-adds | Friday: check shopping list on display, 12 items | At store: open Pantry app on phone, see synced list, check off as shopping

**Preventing Waste:**

- Sunday morning: display shows “⚠️ Spinach expires today, yogurt expires tomorrow” | Suggestions: Green smoothie (uses spinach + yogurt + banana) | Tap recipe, make smoothie, food saved from trash

### Why This Works When Others Don’t

**Recipe apps (Tasty, Yummly):** Show aspirational meals, assume unlimited pantry | No inventory tracking, can’t filter by what you have | Find recipe, missing 5 ingredients, can’t cook tonight | Pantry shows only recipes you can cook NOW.

**Grocery delivery apps (Instacart, Amazon Fresh):** Push purchases, don’t reduce waste | No expiration tracking, food still goes bad | Optimized for buying not using | Pantry optimizes for using what you own.

**Manual inventory systems (spreadsheets, notebooks):** Too much work, nobody maintains it | Get out of date immediately | Require constant manual updates | Pantry updates automatically with receipt scan or saved recipe shortcuts.

**Generic smart displays (Echo Show, Nest Hub):** Show recipes but no inventory awareness | Can’t suggest based on what you have | No expiration tracking, no shopping automation | Pantry integrates inventory + recipes + shopping into one system.

## PART 3: USER RESEARCH

### User Story 1: Sarah, 32, Marketing Manager

$52K income | Lives alone in 1-bedroom apartment | Cooks 4-5 nights/week | Grocery shops Sundays | Throws away ~$20 food weekly (feels guilty)

**The Problem:** Sarah buys fresh produce with good intentions. By Thursday, she’s forgotten what’s in the crisper drawer. Finds wilted spinach, soft tomatoes, moldy berries on Sunday cleanup. She knows she had ingredients for salads but ordered delivery instead because couldn’t remember what vegetables she had. Meal planning Sunday night takes 45 minutes—checking cupboards, Googling recipes, realizing she’s missing ingredients, giving up and making same pasta dish she makes every week.

**Current System:** Takes photo of fridge after shopping. Never looks at photo. Keeps mental note of what she bought. Forgets by Wednesday. Throws away food every Sunday cleanup. Feels guilty about waste but can’t fix it.

**How Pantry Helps:** Scans receipt Sunday after shopping (30 seconds). Wednesday night: looks at display, sees “Spinach expires in 2 days—here are 3 quick recipes.” Makes spinach salad, no food wasted. Thursday: display suggests using tomatoes before they go soft. Friday shopping list auto-generated based on what ran out during week. No more Sunday guilt-cleaning fridge.

**Design takeaway:** Solo users need expiration alerts prominently displayed, quick weeknight recipes emphasized, and visual confirmation that food won’t be wasted.

### User Story 2: Marcus & Lisa, Both 38, Two Kids (Ages 6 & 9)

Combined $68K income | Own home in suburbs | Cook family dinners 6 nights/week | Grocery shop twice weekly | Feed 4 people, waste $40+ food weekly

**The Problem:** Marcus and Lisa can’t coordinate what’s in the pantry. Lisa buys pasta, Marcus buys pasta next day (didn’t know they had it). Kids request specific meals, parents can’t remember if they have ingredients. “What’s for dinner?” conversation every night at 5:30 PM ends in argument because nobody wants to decide. They buy ingredients for ambitious recipes on weekends, never cook them, food expires. Throwing away unused ingredients feels worse when feeding kids on budget.

**Current System:** Shared note on phones for shopping list. Both forget to update it. Buy duplicates. Make same rotation of 7 meals because they know they have ingredients. Kids eat pizza delivery twice a week because parents couldn’t decide fast enough.

**How Pantry Helps:** Both Marcus and Lisa have Pantry app on phones. Either can scan receipt or add items. Display shows “Family dinners this week” based on inventory. Kids ask “Can we have tacos?” Parents check display: yes, have everything. 5:30 PM: display already suggesting “Quick family meals” based on what’s expiring soon and what kids have liked before. Saved recipe shortcut for “Family Tacos” means Marcus can update inventory with one tap after cooking. No duplicate buying (both see inventory on phone at store). Food waste drops, kids eat home-cooked meals, less delivery guilt.

**Design takeaway:** Families need multi-user access, kid-friendly recipe options, duplicate-prevention features, and saved recipe shortcuts for frequent family meals.

### User Story 3: James, 29, Software Developer

$71K income | Lives with roommate | Cooks 3-4 nights/week | Meal preps Sundays for work lunches | Wants to eat healthier, reduce delivery spending

**The Problem:** James follows fitness accounts on Instagram, sees meal prep recipes, screenshots them. Never looks at screenshots again. Cooks all 3 Sunday, marks “Made Chicken Bowls (6 servings)” with one tap—inventory updates automatically. **Design takeaway:** Meal prep users need batch recipe suggestions, shared-ingredient optimization, leftover tracking with expiration dates, and portion counting.

$46K income | Married, one teen daughter | Cook vegetarian meals nightly | Farmer’s market Saturdays + grocery store midweek | Want to support local but farmers market produce goes bad fast

### Final Reminders

This is not fill-in-the-blank work—creativity happens in how you interpret “kitchen helper that reduces waste.” Kitchen tech often looks either sterile/clinical (appliances) or aspirational/unattainable (gourmet recipe apps). Pantry should feel like a practical friend saying “Use the bell peppers before they go bad—here’s how.” Your unique value proposition is **automatic inventory tracking (receipt scan or individual items or saved recipe shortcuts) combined with recipe suggestions based on what you actually have, with expiration alerts that prevent waste**—not fantasy meal planning, not manual tracking nobody maintains, not recipes requiring ingredients you don’t own. Emphasize the complete system: mounted home base for planning and scanning → portable for cooking → automatic updates via shortcuts → waste prevention via alerts → money saved. Every design decision needs rationale. This is portfolio-worthy work demonstrating: IoT product design (smart display with dual modes), sustainability-focused brand positioning, kitchen UX design (cooking-friendly interfaces), behavioral change design (waste reduction, habit formation), multi-device ecosystem (display + phone app).

**Keep asking yourself:**

- Does this make inventory tracking feel effortless (receipt scan, saved shortcuts) not like homework?
- Does the portable mode actually work in real cooking scenarios (hands messy, counter space limited)?
- Are expiration alerts prominent enough to actually prevent waste before food goes bad?
- Would someone use saved recipe shortcuts for frequent meals or still manually update?
- Does the design reduce food waste guilt or just document it?
- Would a busy parent use this on a hectic weeknight when everyone’s hungry?
- Does it feel like a kitchen tool that happens to be smart, or a tech gadget trying to belong in the kitchen?
